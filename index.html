Our Evaluation Framework

Every tool assessed using five concrete criteria. Transparent. Reproducible. Evidence-based.

How We Evaluate Tools

Our framework directly impacts student privacy, security, and academic success.

1. Data Collection

How much personal information does the tool require? Can you use it without creating an account? What information is optional vs. required?

LOW RISK

Asks for minimal personal info. User can access features anonymously or with basic account. No unnecessary fields.

MEDIUM RISK

Requires basic account (name, email). May ask for age/grade. Some tracking of usage patterns.

HIGH RISK

Excessive requests for personal info (SSN, phone, address). Requires full profile for basic use. Aggressive data harvesting.

2. Data Protection & Encryption

Does the site use HTTPS (padlock icon)? Is there a clear privacy policy? Have they had data breaches? Do they encrypt data?

LOW RISK

Clear, transparent privacy policy. Uses HTTPS. Encrypts data in transit. 3rd party security audits available. No unnecessary data retention.

MEDIUM RISK

Has privacy policy but it's complex/unclear. Uses HTTPS. May not be fully transparent about encryption. Limited 3rd party audits.

HIGH RISK

No clear privacy policy or it's buried. No HTTPS. Data transmitted unencrypted. No security certifications. Data breaches in history.

3. Third-Party Data Sharing

Do they sell your data? Who are their 'partners'? Can you opt-out of tracking? How many third-party trackers are present?

LOW RISK

Explicitly states they DO NOT sell data. No ads or minimal, non-invasive ads. No tracking pixels. No unnecessary integrations.

MEDIUM RISK

Unclear about data sharing. May share with 'trusted partners.' Ads present but not excessive. Some tracking cookies.

HIGH RISK

Openly sells data to advertisers. Aggressive ad networks. Heavy tracking/retargeting. Unclear data sharing practices. No opt-out.

4. Legal Compliance (COPPA, FERPA, etc.)

Is it COPPA-compliant (safe for under 13)? Is it FERPA-compliant (protects student records)? What's their privacy policy say about minors?

LOW RISK

Explicitly complies with COPPA (under 13) and FERPA (student records). Privacy-by-design. Age-appropriate safety features.

MEDIUM RISK

States compliance but not well-documented. May only partially follow COPPA/FERPA. Some safety features present.

HIGH RISK

No mention of COPPA/FERPA compliance. Violates student privacy laws. No age-appropriate safeguards. Risky for minors.

5. Academic Integrity Design

Does it teach you or just give answers? Could you plagiarize with this? Does the platform explicitly discourage cheating? Is it designed for learning?

LOW RISK

Designed to TEACH concepts. Guides you to answers (Socratic method). No plagiarism shortcuts. Encourages learning over shortcuts.

MEDIUM RISK

Mix of learning tools and potential shortcuts. Explains concepts but makes answers easily available. Could enable cheating if misused.

HIGH RISK

Designed primarily for plagiarism/cheating. Direct essay writing, answer copying, contract cheating services. No learning value.

Overall Risk Rating Scale

Low Risk

Safe to use. Transparent privacy practices. Designed for learning. Good for students.

Medium Risk

Use with caution. Review privacy settings. Be mindful of how you use it. Follow school guidelines.

High Risk

Avoid if possible. Privacy violations, cheating facilitation, or security issues. Not recommended.

How to Use This Framework

  1. For each tool, evaluate it against all 5 criteria above.
  2. Score each criterion as Low, Medium, or High Risk based on the descriptions.
  3. If 3+ criteria score High Risk, the tool is HIGH RISK overall.
  4. If 2+ criteria score High Risk and others are Mixed, it's MEDIUM RISK.
  5. If most criteria score Low Risk with few exceptions, it's LOW RISK.

KEY PRINCIPLE: This framework is about TRANSPARENCY. We're not saying 'never use medium or high risk tools'—we're helping you make INFORMED CHOICES about where your data goes and how to use tools responsibly.

How to Spot Unsafe Websites

Red Flags to Watch For

Too Many Pop-Ups & Ads: Sketchy sites are filled with aggressive ads and pop-ups.

Asks for Excessive Personal Information: Does the site ask for SSN, credit card, or mother\'s maiden name when it shouldn\'t? Be suspicious.

No Privacy Policy: Every legitimate website has a privacy policy. If you can\'t find one, that\'s a huge red flag.

Suspicious URLs: Check the website address. Scam sites often misspell real domains (like "amaz0n.com").

No HTTPS: Look for the padlock icon. If the site doesn\'t use HTTPS, your data isn\'t protected.

Poor Grammar & Spelling: Professional sites have polished content. Multiple errors indicate an unprofessional site.

Promises That Sound Too Good: Free money, guaranteed grades? Probably a scam.

No Contact Information: Real companies have ways to contact them.

What to Do

When you encounter a suspicious website, simply leave and find an alternative. Trust your instincts—if something feels off, it probably is.

Understanding Data Collection

What Is Data Collection?

Data collection is when apps, websites, and services gather information about you—your location, browsing habits, age, interests, device info, and more.

Why Do Companies Collect Data?

To Sell to Advertisers: Your data is valuable. Companies sell it so advertisers can target you.

To Improve Their Product: Understanding how you use an app helps them make it better.

To Make Profit: Data is currency.

What Data Is Collected?

Your clicks, searches, location, device info, email, sometimes deleted messages. Apps collect data even when you\'re not using them.

Who Has Access?

The company, advertisers, third-party analytics firms, sometimes hackers (if there\'s a breach).

What You Can Do

Read Privacy Policies: Know what you\'re agreeing to.

Request Your Data: Many companies let you request what they have about you.

Limit Tracking: Turn off location, disable cookies, limit permissions.

Use Privacy Tools: VPNs, ad blockers, privacy-focused browsers help.

The Bigger Picture

If a free app has no obvious way to make money, you might be the product—your data is being sold.

Critical Thinking with AI

Why AI Gets Things Wrong

AI models are trained on patterns in text, not on understanding truth. They can confidently state false information. This is called "hallucinating."

How to Verify AI Outputs

Cross-Check with Reliable Sources: If ChatGPT claims something, verify it with academic sources, peer-reviewed studies.

Check Citations: If AI cites a source, verify that source actually exists and says what AI claims.

Use Multiple Sources: Don\'t rely on AI alone. Use library databases, academic papers, expert sources.

Know Its Knowledge Cutoff: ChatGPT\'s training data is from April 2024. Current events may not be included.

Red Flags in AI Responses

Overly Confident Tone: Be suspicious of AI stating opinions as facts.

Vague Citations: If it cites a source but can\'t give you a link, verify independently.

Contradicts Multiple Sources: If AI contradicts reputable sources, trust the reputable sources.

The Critical Question

Ask yourself: "Would a human expert agree with this?"

Bottom Line

AI is a tool, not a source of truth. Always verify important information through multiple sources.
